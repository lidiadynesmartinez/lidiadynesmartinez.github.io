AMUN

MEDICAL SPIROMETER for USE IN low income settings

This project involves designing a low-cost spirometer to measure the tidal and residual volumes of patients’ lungs in low income countries, allowing data collection for Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease in countries where little data is available.

 

Areas explored over the course of the project were context and use of design, user experience, product form, ergonomics, transport, technology, manufacture, and maintenance. These ideas were realised through a look-a-like prototype and a work-a-like prototype (functioning technology capable of measuring only tidal breath volume).

 

The project was developed with close collaboration with the Royal Brompton (Mr Simon Ward: Head of Lung Respiratory Unit) and Professor. Peter Burnley (School of Public Health).

PROJECT BRIEF

The design challenge involves designing a portable device that:

  • Can measure tidal lung volume while performing quiet breathing
  • Be used in a low income setting
  • Produce a fully working prototype
  • Can measure reserve lung volume
  • Should be non-invasive
  • Is micro-biologically safe

COUNTRY OF INTEREST - INDIA

Around 104 million people are living in the slums of India and this number is growing each year. This leads to new health conditions due to over-crowding, poor housing and an unsanitary environment coupled with poverty. In order to design a device suitable for use in these environments, the culture and society of the country must be considered:

 

  • Indian culture prefers to treat patients holistically, emphasising prevention.
  • The caste system is still common in poorer parts of the country, those at the bottom of the social ladder require the most help.
  • Women will typically only come to see a doctor if they are also female.
  • Slums tend to have poor vehicle accessibility so the device must be lightweight and portable.

USER TESTING

The design process started with low cost, fast blue foam models. These were tested using people with different hand sizes, ethnicities, ages and positions of holding the device to see how they reacted to the product and whether it was intuitive. When testing the first physical model, features of the design were changed based on user feedback.
